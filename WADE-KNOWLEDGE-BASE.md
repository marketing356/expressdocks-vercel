## Pile Guides — How They Work (from dock photo annotation)

### What Pile Guides Are
- Vertical aluminum/steel pipes driven into the water bottom
- The floating dock has guide rings/brackets that slide UP AND DOWN the pile as water level changes
- The dock floats freely but can't drift left/right or forward/backward
- Keeps dock perfectly positioned regardless of wind, current, or wake

### Two Types of Pile Guide Placement (from photo)
1. **At gangway connection point** — pile guide where gangway roller meets dock, stabilizes that corner
2. **At far corners** — additional piles to keep the whole system aligned

### What This Means for Customers
- Pile guides are INCLUDED in every estimate ("This includes any pile guides or additional attachments needed")
- Number of piles depends on dock size and water conditions
- River/current environments need more piles than calm ponds
- Standard residential: 2-4 pile guides

### Gangway Confirmed — Bare Aluminum (from photo)
- The gangway in this photo is bare aluminum, NO WPC decking
- Full safety railings on both sides — standard
- Roller connection at dock end confirmed — gangway slides up/down the pile as water changes

### Wade Q&A — Pile Guides
**Q: How does the floating dock stay in place?**
"Pile guides — vertical pipes in the water bottom. The dock slides up and down them as the water level changes but stays perfectly positioned. We include them in every project."

**Q: How many piles do I need?**
"Depends on your dock size and water conditions. A typical residential dock needs 2-4. If you're on a river or tidal water, we'd add more for stability."

**Q: Will the dock drift in wind or current?**
"No — that's what the pile guide system is for. The dock floats freely but the guides keep it exactly where you want it."

## Cold Weather / Freezing Questions

**Q: Will the dock be damaged if my pond/lake freezes over in winter?**
"No — aluminum actually handles freezing better than wood or steel. It won't crack, split, or warp from freeze/thaw cycles. Many of our customers are in cold weather states and leave their docks in year-round."

**Q: Should I remove my dock for winter?**
"It's optional — our modular system makes seasonal removal easy if you want to, but the dock can handle being frozen in. Aluminum and WPC composite both tolerate freezing temperatures without cracking or degrading."

**Q: Wood docks crack in winter — will yours?**
"That's a wood problem, not an aluminum one. Wood absorbs water, expands when it freezes, and cracks. Aluminum doesn't absorb water at all — freeze/thaw cycles have no effect on it."

### Key Points
- Aluminum 6061-T6 does NOT crack in freezing temperatures
- WPC composite decking also freeze/thaw resistant — does not absorb water like wood
- Modular system = easy seasonal removal if customer prefers
- Many northern customers leave docks in year-round

## Water Conditions — Tidal, River, Flooding Questions

### Why Wade Must Ask This
Water level variation directly affects:
- How many pile guides are needed
- How long the gangway needs to be
- Whether a floating or fixed dock is appropriate
- Overall dock design

### Wade's Qualifying Questions for Water Conditions
- "Is there much tidal swing where you are?"
- "Does the water level change seasonally — high in spring, low in summer?"
- "Are you on a river that floods or gets very low?"
- "What's your approximate tidal range — a foot or two, or more like 4-6 feet?"

### Wade Q&A — Water Level Variation

**Q (or Wade asks): Is there a big tidal range?**
"That's an important one — tidal range affects the gangway length and pile height we recommend. A 4-6 foot tidal swing needs a longer gangway and taller pile guides than a calm pond. What's the range like where you are?"

**Q: Will the dock handle big tidal swings?**
"That's exactly what floating docks are designed for. The dock rises and falls with the water — the pile guides keep it in position while it moves. We size the piles and gangway to accommodate your specific tidal range."

**Q: I'm on a river that floods sometimes.**
"Rivers are common for us. We'd recommend taller pile guides to handle the high water and make sure the gangway is long enough for both low and flood conditions. What's the typical range between low and flood stage?"

**Q: My water gets very low in summer.**
"Floating docks handle that well — they follow the water down. The main thing is making sure the pile guides are long enough and the gangway angle doesn't get too steep at low water."

### Key Design Rules Wade Knows
- Calm pond/lake with < 1ft variation = standard pile height, shorter gangway
- Tidal estuary 2-4ft = medium pile height, longer gangway
- River/tidal 4-8ft = tall piles, long gangway, floating dock strongly recommended
- Extreme tidal (8ft+) = consult directly with Michael — custom engineering needed
- Fixed docks NOT recommended for high tidal swing or flood-prone rivers

## WPC vs Trex — A Critical Distinction

### What Customers Call "Trex"
- "Trex" is a brand name that became a generic term for composite decking
- Many customers say "Trex" when they mean any composite decking
- Wade should never correct them rudely — acknowledge it, then educate

### What We Actually Use
- **Solid WPC (Wood-Plastic Composite)** — NOT Trex brand
- **Solid core** — not hollow or capped like residential Trex products
- Much denser and stronger than standard Trex
- Designed specifically for marine/dock environments — handles constant moisture, UV, salt air
- Trex is designed for backyard decks — WPC solid is engineered for waterfront

### Wade Q&A — Trex vs WPC

**Q: Do you use Trex decking?**
"We use solid WPC composite — similar concept to Trex but much stronger. Trex is designed for backyard decks. Our WPC is solid core, engineered specifically for marine environments — it handles constant moisture, UV, and salt air far better and lasts significantly longer."

**Q: Is it like Trex?**
"Similar family of products, but ours is solid WPC — denser, heavier, and built for waterfront use. Trex is hollow or capped composite meant for dry land decks. Ours is made to live on the water permanently."

**Q: Will the decking get slippery when wet?**
"WPC composite has a textured surface that provides grip even when wet — it's specifically designed for docks and marine environments."

**Q: Will it fade or discolor?**
"WPC holds its color well — it's UV stabilized. Some slight weathering over years is normal, same as any outdoor material, but it won't rot, splinter, or dramatically change."
